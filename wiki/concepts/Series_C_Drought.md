# Series C Drought

## Definition

The severe contraction in late-stage (Series C+) venture funding in India, with 2025 levels falling back to 2017 volumes (~$5B). This creates a structural gap where companies that have raised Series A and B cannot access private growth capital and are forced into alternative paths — primarily premature IPOs.

## Scale of the Problem

- Series C capital deployed in India in 2025 is at approximately 2017 levels (~$5B)
- 90% of listed value in India is still traditional companies — [[Source: Breaking Down India's IPO Wave 3, The Series C Drought]]

## Root Cause: Crossover Funds Disappeared

The Series C+ market in India (2012-2024) was dominated by global crossover funds investing in India. These have almost entirely withdrawn:
- **8 Roads** (Fidelity's balance sheet arm) announced merging into a single Asia-Pacific team — no more India-specific investment team
- Multiple billion-dollar-plus funds have quietly stopped investing in India — these closures don't make headlines the way startup shutdowns do
- The international pools of capital are no longer excited about Series B/C in India
- Risk-adjusted returns at Series C+ were "always very poor" due to valuation risk — SoftBank/NPF entering Meesho at $5B got only ~10% IRR if IPO at $6-7B

This is structural, not cyclical — [[Source: Breaking Down India's IPO Wave 3, The Series C Drought]]

## Second-Order Effects

1. **Premature IPOs:** Companies that might need 2-3 more years of private growth are being pushed to list because they can't raise Series C. The IPO window is open, so "you can't time the market" — better to go now than risk the window closing.
2. **Compressed growth timelines:** Companies born 2015-2020 are reaching IPO faster (10-year cycle) vs 2008-2015 cohort (12-15 years). The goal is to compress further to 8-10 years.
3. **Profitability pressure:** Without late-stage capital to fund growth, companies must become profitable earlier, which actually makes them better IPO candidates.
4. **Fund size constraints:** Indian VC funds struggle to grow larger because 3x in 10 years (~13-15% IRR) isn't compelling enough for LPs. The 5x/10-year norm needs to become baseline (~25% IRR accounting for drawdown schedules). 4x gross only gives 3x net after fees and carry — [[Source: Breaking Down India's IPO Wave 3, The Series C Drought]].
5. **Domestic capital filling the gap:** New Indian GPs ([[Kedara Capital]], A91, Create, Multiples) are emerging, but need domestic family offices as LPs. The opportunity is in Series B-C where risk is now lower and IPO path is 5 years not 10 — [[Source: Breaking Down India's IPO Wave 3, The Series C Drought]].
6. **Secondary market dysfunction:** Secondary funds need minimum ₹50-100 Cr tickets, but sellers prefer to wait 18-24 months for IPO rather than sell at a private market discount. Private markets value on 6-month forward (FY26), public markets on 18-24 month forward (FY27-28) — so waiting pays — [[Source: Breaking Down India's IPO Wave 3, The Series C Drought]].
