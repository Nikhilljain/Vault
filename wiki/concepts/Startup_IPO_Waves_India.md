# Startup IPO Waves India

## Definition

The cyclical pattern of Indian startup IPOs, characterized by three distinct waves driven by market conditions, retail participation, and venture fund liquidity needs.

## The Three Waves

### Wave 1 (~2021)
- COVID-era liquidity boom drove a surge of startup listings
- Many companies listed at peak valuations
- Market corrected sharply post-listing for several names
- [[Zomato]] was a landmark listing that inspired more startups to pursue IPO

### Wave 2 (~2023-2024)
- Quieter period; market recovered but startup IPOs were selective
- [[Mobikwik]] attempted three times before finally listing

### Wave 3 (~2025 — Current)
- Looks "more solid" than Wave 1 — more companies have clear profitability paths
- 16 startup IPOs in 2025; 7 already listed, 14 more filed
- Pipeline is ~3.5x current listed count
- **Big 5 upcoming:** [[Lenskart]] (Nov 2025), [[Meesho]] (SEBI approved, ~$6-7B), [[Groww]], [[boAt]], [[Razorpay]]
- TRMG startup index outperforming Nifty and S&P 500
- IPO window has been open 18-24 months — historically rare
- SEBI filing-to-listing timeline reduced from 12-18 months to 3-4 months
- Driven by: strong retail SIP flows, more profitable companies, [[Series C Drought]] forcing IPO route

All Wave 3 data from [[Source: Breaking Down India's IPO Wave 3, The Series C Drought]]

## Startups Are a Drop in the Ocean

Startups are only **3-4% by number** and **10-15% by value** of total IPOs in India. 90% of IPO value is still traditional companies. The non-startup IPO market is massive — example: a B2B plastic manufacturer for auto OEMs with ₹25-30 Cr revenue filing at ₹350 Cr valuation. Newspaper front pages are full of IPO ads for companies nobody has heard of. Gold is up 70% in 1 year while Nifty is flat — [[Source: Breaking Down India's IPO Wave 3, The Series C Drought]].

## Key Tension

Two-thirds of startups that went public are NOT yet profitable. Yet public markets are rewarding them because retail capital is abundant and the startup index is outperforming. The question is whether this is sustainable or whether it mirrors 2021's irrational exuberance — this time the hosts argue the underlying companies are stronger.

## Structural Impact

Each wave creates a flywheel: successful IPOs → wealth creation → more people inspired to start companies → more angel/venture capital → more startups → more IPOs. The [[Flipkart]] acquisition and [[Zomato]] listing were key catalysts for earlier waves — [[Source: Breaking Down India's IPO Wave 3, The Series C Drought]].
